# Episode 2: After the Visit

**POV:** ELIJAH
**Act:** 1

## Synopsis
After leaving Camille's house, Elijah is forced into the first truly unguarded reckoning of his life. What begins as shock over her devastation turns into something more corrosive: the realization that the story he told himself about love, sacrifice, and destiny may have only been a mask for cowardice, appetite, and self-deception. As he moves from the woman he chose to the mother who no longer recognizes him, the episode strips away every justification he has been hiding behind, leaving him face to face with the unbearable truth that the cost of what he wanted was not only Camille's ruin, but the destruction of the man he believed himself to be.

## Episode Text

POV: ELIJAH

I sat in the car for forty minutes before I could drive.

Not because I was crying, though I was, in that silent, structural way where the tears just fall without your permission, without sound, without the relief that crying is supposed to bring. I sat in the car because my hands wouldn't work. I'd grip the steering wheel, and then my fingers would go slack, like the signal between my brain and my body had been severed. Like the man who had walked into that kitchen was not the same man sitting in this car, and the new one didn't know how to operate the machinery yet.

She looked at me the way you look at furniture.

Not with hate. I could have handled hate. Hate is engagement. Hate means you still care enough to feel something toward the person who hurt you. What I saw in Camille's eyes was something I don't have a word for. Vacancy, maybe. The particular emptiness of a building after it's been condemned—still standing, still technically intact, but hollowed out.

I carry mine alone.

The sentence sat in my chest like a stone. Because she was right. That was the obscenity of it—she was completely, precisely, devastatingly right. I would have someone to hold when the weight of what I'd done became unbearable. And she would take her five o'clock pill and call the children and sit in her parents' kitchen and survive another evening alone because I had taken every other option from her.

I finally started the car. Drove without seeing, the highway unspooling beneath me while my mind played her voice on a loop that flat, steady, terrifying voice. The voice of a woman who had moved past grief into something that didn't have a name. Something clinical. Something that scared me more than her silence ever had.

She'd been hospitalized. An emergency room visit that Daniela had covered up. And I hadn't known. Hadn't even suspected. Had been so consumed by what I'd found, so intoxicated by what I'd mistaken for depth, that I'd missed the woman I loved nearly dying.

What kind of man misses that?

The house was dark when I got home. She was in the kitchen, her usual spot, reviewing something on her laptop, a glass of wine half-finished beside her. She looked up when I came in, and I watched her face shift from casual greeting to concern as she registered whatever was showing on mine.

"How was she?" she asked carefully.

I stood in the doorway and looked at her. Really looked at her—this woman I'd upended my entire life for, who I'd told myself was worth any cost. Her careful posture. Her analytical eyes. The way she was already calculating how to manage my emotional state, the way she managed everything with precision, with strategy, with that particular brand of competence that I'd once mistaken for depth.

"She almost died," I said.

Her hand stilled on her wine glass.

"She was hospitalized. An overdose. Daniela covered it up with some story about a conference. And we didn't notice. We were so busy in our own world that we didn't notice she was dying."

"Elijah—"

"Don't." The word came out harder than I intended. Or maybe exactly as hard as I intended. "Don't say my name in that careful voice. Don't manage this. Don't apply your analytical framework to the fact that the woman I loved nearly killed herself because of what we did."

She set down the glass. "What we did," she repeated quietly. "Or what we are?"

And there it was. The question that had been living underneath everything for months, the one neither of us had had the courage to look at directly. Not what happened between us, but what we really were. Not the narrative we'd constructed—profound, complete, worth any sacrifice—but the truth underneath the narrative.

I pulled out a chair and sat across from her. The kitchen was beautiful—her taste, mostly, that clean contemporary aesthetic that photographed well and felt like living inside a magazine. Nothing like the kitchen Camille and I had built together. Nothing like the warm, complicated, lived-in spaces she created wherever she went.

"Who am I?" I asked.

She blinked. "What?"

"Who am I? Right now, sitting here. Who is this person?" I pressed my palms flat on the table, needing something solid. "Because the man I was, the man who loved Camille, who built a family with her, who would have done anything to protect her, that man would never have done this. Would never have let this happen. Would never have been so blinded by his own desire that he watched the woman he loved disappear and did nothing."

"You're being too hard on yourself."

"I'm not being hard enough." My voice cracked, and I let it. "I sat in that kitchen today and looked at what's left of her. She's…she's a ghost. She's hollow. She takes five pills a day just to get from morning to night. She told me the only thing that kept her from swallowing a bottle of sleeping pills was the children. The children. Not us. Not love. Not hope. Just the logistical fact that someone needed to sign permission slips and show up at recitals."

She was quiet for a long moment. "What do you want me to say?"

"I want you to tell me the truth. For once, I want the truth instead of some…strategy." I leaned forward. "Are you a good person?"

The question landed. I watched her absorb it, watched the careful mask slip just slightly before she rebuilt it. That was the thing about her: she was always rebuilding, always reconstructing, always managing the narrative. Even now. Even in this.

"I don't think that's a useful question," she said.

"It's the only question that matters." I stood up because sitting felt like agreement, felt like continuing to participate in something I was beginning to see clearly for the first time. "You're not a good person. And I don't say that with cruelty. I say it because I need to finally be honest about what I've been building my life around."

"Elijah "

"You pursued this. You wanted this. You saw an opportunity, and you took it with the same precision you bring to every acquisition, every hostile takeover, every carefully executed strategy. And I let you. I let you because what you offered felt…"

I stopped. Pressed my hands to my face. Because the next words required admitting something I'd been refusing to see.

"It felt profound," I said into my palms. "And I wanted it to be profound. I needed it to be profound, because the alternative was admitting that I'd destroyed the best thing in my life for something ordinary."

The silence that followed was different from the silence in Camille's kitchen. That one had been heavy with absence. This one was heavy with presence, with the accumulated weight of everything we'd told ourselves to justify what we'd done.

"So what are you saying?" she asked. Her voice had gone careful. Strategic. The voice she used when things were falling apart.

"I'm saying there's no forever here." The words came out tired rather than dramatic. "We both know that. We've both known it for a while, in the part of ourselves that isn't performing this great love story. You're not good for me. This isn't good for me. And I am not the man I was before you, and I don't like who I've become."

"You're in shock from seeing her. You're not thinking clearly. "

"I'm thinking clearly for the first time in a year." I dropped my hands. Looked at her. "Who were we pretending for? Who was the audience for this grand passion? Because Camille's gone. The children look at us with something I can't even name. My mother doesn't recognize me when I call. So who is this performance for?"

She didn't answer. For once, the woman who always had an answer, a strategy, a framework, a carefully constructed response, had nothing.

"I'm going for a drive," I said. "Don't wait up."

I called my mother from a parking lot off Route 1. Eleven-thirty at night, the engine idling, the empty highway stretching in both directions.

She answered on the third ring. My mother always answers, no matter the hour, because she's spent sixty-seven years being the person people call when they've run out of other options. But the voice that said hello wasn't the voice I remembered—wasn't the steady, certain, here-is-what-you-do voice that had guided me through every crisis of my life. It was tentative. Confused. The voice of a woman trying to help a son she no longer recognizes.

"It's me, Mama."

"I know." A pause. The sound of her adjusting in bed, of my father's muffled voice asking who it was, of her whispering go back to sleep, it's the boy. The boy. That's what she still called me. As if I were still someone she could identify.

"I saw Camille today."

Another pause. Longer. My mother loved Camille—not in the careful, diplomatic way mothers-in-law sometimes love their sons' wives, but genuinely, completely. Camille had learned to cook her recipes. Had called her every Sunday. Had put her grandchildren on the phone and made sure they spoke to her in full sentences, not the distracted half-answers children sometimes give. When everything fell apart, my mother hadn't yelled at me or delivered ultimatums. She'd just gone very quiet, and the quiet had been worse.

"How is she?" my mother asked, and even in those three words I could hear it—the love for Camille, the bewilderment at her own son, the helplessness of a woman who raised a man she thought she knew and now wasn't sure.

"She's not good, Mama."

"No," my mother agreed softly. "She wouldn't be."

I pressed my forehead to the steering wheel. "I don't know who I am anymore."

The silence on her end lasted long enough that I wondered if she'd hung up. Then: "I don't either, baby. I've been trying. When you call, I try to hear my son in your voice. The boy who used to sit at my table and tell me about his plans, who knew exactly who he was and what he wanted. And I can't find him."

"Mama "

"I'm not saying this to hurt you. I'm saying it because you called me at midnight, and I think you called because you need someone to tell you the truth." Her voice was shaking now. "I don't know how to help you. I have never not known how to help you. You are my child, and I would walk through fire for you, and I don't know how to help you because I don't know who I'm helping."

I was crying. The kind of crying that comes from somewhere below the chest, below the stomach, from some deep foundation that's finally giving way.

"The man who loved Camille," my mother continued quietly. "That man—I knew him. I raised him. I was proud of him every day of his life. Whatever this is now, whoever you've become, I love you because you're mine. But I don't know this person. And I think...I think maybe you don't either."

"No," I whispered. "I don't."

"Then maybe that's where you start." Her voice had found something—not the old certainty, but something gentler, something that came from the same place. "Not with her. Not with what you think you've found. But with figuring out who you are when all the justifications are stripped away. When it's just you and the truth about what you've done."

"What if the truth is that I destroyed the best person I've ever known for something that isn't even real?"

My mother was quiet for a long time. In the background, I could hear the sounds of her house—the clock in the hallway, the refrigerator humming, the steady creaks of a home that had sheltered fifty years of family life. Sounds that meant safety. That meant foundation. That meant something you could trust.

"Then you live with that," she said finally. "And you spend the rest of your life trying to be worthy of knowing it."

She didn't say goodnight. She just stayed on the line, breathing, while I sat in a parking lot and cried. The way she'd stayed when I was a child and woke from nightmares—not fixing anything, not offering solutions, just being present while the darkness passed.

Except this time, I wasn't sure it would pass. And I think she knew that.

I drove for two more hours. Not toward anything. Just driving, the way you do when your body needs movement because stillness has become unbearable. The highway was empty at this hour—just me and the trucks and the particular loneliness of someone who has everything he thought he wanted and has never been less sure of who he is.

Who were we pretending for?

The question wouldn't leave. Because I'd asked it to hurt her, but it had cut me deeper. The grand narrative, the profound connection, the thing that was worth any cost, the love that existed independently and completely. When had I stopped believing it? When had the performance of it become more real than the thing itself?

Maybe when Camille stopped answering my calls. Maybe when the children started looking through me. Maybe when I realized that what I'd felt in those months away was indistinguishable from what I'd felt for her, and if it was interchangeable, then maybe it wasn't as singular as I'd needed it to be.

Or maybe tonight. In that kitchen. Looking at the wreckage of a woman who had loved me with everything she had, and knowing with the same certainty I used to know anything that the man she'd loved had not done this. Could not have done this. That in the process of destroying Camille's life, I had become someone else entirely. Someone my mother couldn't recognize. Someone I couldn't recognize.

I pulled over. Cut the engine. Sat in the dark on the shoulder of a highway and let the silence have me.

There was nothing left to perform. No profound identity to justify, no grand narrative to maintain, no strategic framework to impose on the mess I'd made. There was just this: a man in a car, on the side of a road, finally understanding that the cost of what he'd wanted was the person he used to be.

And that the person he used to be was the only one worth being.
