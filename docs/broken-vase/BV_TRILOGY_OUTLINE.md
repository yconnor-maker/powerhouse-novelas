### Book 1 — Key Decisions (Settled)
- Jessica is never named in Book 1. The reader suspects but doesn't know.
- The affair partner pronouns have been swapped (he/him → she/her) to encode Jessica as female throughout.
- Book 1 releases as a standalone. The open ending creates demand.
- Compression is the craft. Do not pad.

### Book 2 — Key Arcs
1. **The women's relationship:** Tension → exhaustion → acceptance. Not friendship. Not forgiveness. Just the slow, grudging recognition that they are permanent in each other's lives and something has to give.
2. **The palimpsest:** Flashback chapters running the same timeline as Book 1, revealing what was underneath. How the affair started. The girlhood. The mothers' rivalry migrating into their daughters.
3. **The mothers:** Catherine and Rachel. The sisters whose competition seeded everything. Catherine blaming Rachel. Rachel unable to look anyone in the eye. The generational weight.
4. **Jessica's reckoning:** She wanted to be the star. She wanted the validation. She wanted proof she could have what Camille had. Now she has a child and no man and a family that can barely look at her. Who is she outside of what she did?
5. **Camille's reckoning:** The abandoned career picked back up. The woman who forgives because she was taught to — is that strength or is it the most elegant form of surrender?

## BOOK 3: [TITLE TBD]
*"What staying actually looks like"*

**POV:** All three — Camille, Elijah, Jessica. First person, rotating.
**Timeline:** Post-baptism forward. The hardest year. What a family looks like when it's been rebuilt with someone else's child in it.
**Craft move:** This is the payoff book. Three voices. The technically challenging one. The reader now has all three interiors and can see the full architecture of the story.
**Status:** Conceptual. Key themes identified.

### Arc Sketches

**Camille's arc:**
- She has already proven she can live without Elijah. Kevin proved that. She stays because he is home — not because she needs him but because she chooses him. That distinction is the load-bearing wall of the entire trilogy.
- The career she picked back up in Book 2 is now real. The community architecture firm. The work that has meaning. She's no longer a woman defined by her husband's life.
- The question of the child. She said the child would be welcome, and she meant it. But what does that actually look like? A baby in the house that looks like your husband and was made with someone else. The daily reality of that promise.
- Her relationship with Jessica evolves — not into friendship, but into something that doesn't have a name. Two women who share a man's children. Co-mothers. Co-survivors. Something new.

**Elijah's arc:**
- His redemption is real but permanently scarred. He is a good father to this child. He is a faithful husband. He has not betrayed Camille again and will not. But the scar is load-bearing — it's part of the structure now.
- The permanent consequence: Jessica is in his life forever. Every co-parenting handoff, every birthday, every school event — she's there. And he has to navigate that while keeping Camille's trust.
- Flashbacks: how his view of Jessica shifted over the years. The slow migration from noticing her at family events to the first conversation that went too long to the night it crossed the line. Not to excuse it — to understand it. The ordinary human capacity to want two things at once and choose wrong.
- What intimacy looks like through continued almost-betrayal — not another affair, but the scar being permanent. The flinch that never fully goes away. The moment in bed where Camille goes quiet and he knows she's somewhere else, measuring, testing, and he has to lie still and let her find her way back.

**Jessica's arc:**
- Navigating motherhood alone. Not pitied — she doesn't want pity. But alone in a way she never planned for.
- Who is she outside of the thing she did? The accomplished, sophisticated, star-of-the-room Jessica — is she still that person? Or was that person always performing, and motherhood is the first thing that's ever been real?
- Her relationship with her child. The love that exists independent of the circumstances. The fierce, immediate, non-negotiable love that arrives whether you earned it or not.
- Her relationship with Camille. The slow, impossible, necessary evolution from enemy to something else. Not friends. Something harder. Something that requires more courage than either of them thought they had.
- The question she's been avoiding since Book 1: did she love Elijah? Really love him? Or was it recognition, competition, loneliness, desire? What did she call it, and was she right?

### Book 3 — Key Scenes (Conceptual)
- The first co-parenting handoff. Elijah at Jessica's door with a car seat. The banality of it.
- A holiday — Thanksgiving or Christmas — where both families are present. Catherine and Rachel in the same room. The children playing together without understanding the architecture of damage above their heads.
- An intimacy scene between Camille and Elijah where the scar shows. Where she stops. Where he waits. Where they find their way back — or don't.
- Jessica alone with the baby at 3 AM. The silence. The particular loneliness of single motherhood when you can't even be angry at anyone but yourself.
- A scene where Camille and Jessica are alone together for the first time since the doorstep. Not planned — circumstantial. And the thing that passes between them is not what either expected.
- The ending. What staying actually looks like. Not the fairy tale. Not the destruction. Something in between. Something real. Something that makes the reader put down the book and sit with it.

### Book 3 — Still Needs
- Everything. Full dictation sessions. This is the hardest book to write.
- Jessica's full voice locked (her metaphor system, her register — what's hers the way architecture is Camille's and finance is Elijah's?)
- The structure: is it chronological or does it move in time? Does it cover a year? Two years? Until the child is walking?
- The ending. What is the last image of the trilogy? Where does the reader leave these people?
