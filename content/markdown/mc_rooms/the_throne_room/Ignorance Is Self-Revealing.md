Ignorance Is Self-Revealing

I don’t correct every misreading of me.

Not because I don’t notice it. Not because I’m above it. And definitely not because I’m confused about who I am.

I don’t correct it because people who misunderstand you will usually tell on themselves—quickly, loudly, and with far more honesty than they ever intended.

There’s a particular kind of arrogance that shows up when someone believes you are decorative. Harmless. A soft accessory to a room that belongs to them. They loosen their grip on discipline. They stop editing themselves. They start speaking the way they speak when they’re sure the person in front of them doesn’t count.

And that is when you learn the truth.

Most women have seen the cleanest version of this in professional life: you say something direct and usable in a meeting, and it lands like air. Then a man repeats the same idea with slightly different cadence, and suddenly it’s brilliance. The common mistake is thinking the problem is your delivery. The real problem is that the room does not want to grant you authorship.

So here is what I do instead.

I watch.

Because when people misread you, they behave differently around you—and behavior is always more valuable than opinion. Behavior is data. Behavior is a confession.

I’ve been at dinners where someone spoke to me like I was merely the atmosphere. They asked polite questions that required nothing of them. They made small jokes that assumed I would smile instead of respond. They offered opinions with that casual certainty people only use when they believe no one is keeping track.

And I let them.

Not forever. Not in every context. But long enough.

Long enough to hear what they think of the people in the room who aren’t present. Long enough to hear what they really believe about women. Long enough to understand who they flatter and who they fear. Long enough to see what they say when they think they’re safe.

Ignorance is self-revealing because it makes people lazy.

They stop guarding their motives. They stop hiding their contempt. They stop pretending they’re fair. And all I have to do is sit there—quiet, composed, uninterested in rescuing them from their own assumptions.

That’s the part most people don’t understand: correcting someone too early is sometimes an act of generosity you don’t owe.

Clarity is not neutral. Clarity changes the temperature in a room. Clarity can escalate a dynamic. Clarity can trigger insecurity in the kind of person who experiences being corrected as humiliation. There are environments where the cost of being “understood” is higher than the benefit.

So I clarify selectively.

I clarify when it serves me. When it protects something I value. When the outcome matters. When the audience is someone whose perception has actual consequences in my life. When I am choosing truth as a tool—not truth as performance.

Because there is also a quiet kind of power in letting a misreader keep misreading.

People who underestimate you make different decisions around you. They take risks they shouldn’t. They show you their strategy. They expose the gap between who they pretend to be and who they are when they think no one important is watching.

Sometimes the only way to see someone clearly is to let them believe they’re the one holding the advantage.

And it isn’t just about men. Women do this to each other, too—especially the women who have trained themselves to survive by becoming legible, agreeable, endlessly explanatory.
